      Meet the Super Bowl 2016 player from New Jersey who saved his sister's life | Politi

      Print Email
      Steve Politi | NJ Advance Media for NJ.com By Steve Politi | NJ Advance Media for NJ.com The Star-Ledger
      Email the author | Follow on Twitter
      on February 03, 2016 at 6:13 AM, updated February 03, 2016 at 7:33 AM
      comments

      SAN JOSE, Calif. — The father had so much on his mind as he worked his way up the New Jersey Turnpike that day in 2001. His daughter, still just a toddler, had a rare blood disease with little hope for a cure. His son, an active fourth-grade boy, was driving with him to Hackensack University Medical Center preparing to donate his bone marrow to save her.

      Scott Simonson was alternately dealing with his worst fears and his greatest hopes as a father when the boy in the passenger seat asked him a question.

      "Who is going to get my room?"

      The father was confused. He asked what the boy meant.

      "I'm going to die. Who is going to get my stuff?"

      Simonson pulled the car to the side of the Turnpike in disbelief. He realized his son, Scott Jr., thought he was giving up his own life to save his 3-year-old sister, Shannon, that day. But it was the next realization that made him fight back tears 15 years later.

      The boy didn't care.

      MORE: Who are the 50 greatest NFL players from New Jersey? 

      "There is nothing my son can do on the football field — EVER! — will compare to what he has done already for this family," the father said from Red Bank this week. "This weekend could be God giving my son a high five for what he did, for everything that happened."

      Because this weekend is, and against long odds on an athletic level — we'll get to that part of this Jersey underdog story soon enough — Scott Simonson will take the field as a special teams player and a third-string tight end for the favored Carolina Panthers. 

      Brandon Marshall on being confused with the other Brandon Marshall Broncos linebacker Brandon Marshall gets asked by Leon from Curb Your Enthusiasm about being constantly confused with the other Brandon Marshall.

      He certainly won't attract much attention. He won't, in fact, even be the most famous Carolina tight end from New Jersey, not with Wayne Hills star Greg Olsen on his team. 

      But the Red Bank native who played for powerhouse Middletown South will be there at Levi's Stadium, and so will a family that includes the sick little girl he helped save. Shannon is now a high school junior who just got her driver's license with no signs of the disease that might have killed her.

      The father considers himself the luckiest man on earth. Oh, yeah, and he also gets to watch his son in the Super Bowl on Sunday.

      A scary diagnosis 

      The Simonsons knew something wasn't right. They were at the doctor's office for what was supposed to be a routine physical for their youngest daughter, Shannon, who had just turned 1. But the doctor kept redoing the blood test. 

      Then came the diagnosis: Shannon had an extremely rare genetic condition called Fanconi Anemia. There were only 1,800 known cases in the world, and for most, the outcome was grim. Many patients with the blood disorder would not live to adulthood. 

      The family was devastated. Soon, however, they would discover a series of breaks that Scott Simonson Sr. said "were about as likely as winning the Lotto every year of my life."

      First, the leading doctor for Fanconi Anemia was right in New Jersey: Alfred Gillio, a pediatric hematologist/oncologist in Hackensack. Gillio had begun working with a new drug to treat the condition, one that his patients could receive without the devastating affects of radiation treatment on their young bodies. 

      PLUS: New Jersey legend Sam Mills Jr. still inspires the Panthers 

      But they needed a bone marrow donor. Simonson Sr. remembers the look of amazement on the doctor's face when he delivered the news. Not only was his son a perfect match, but so was the family's oldest daughter, Kelsey.

      "They were very lucky," Gillio said from his office, and there was never really any doubt. Scott Jr., or Scotty as the family called him, would be the donor. "Being the typical big brother," he said, "I was all for it."

      Rashad Jennings Dances To Justin Bieber Rashad Jennings Dances To Justin Bieber

      His father was amazed that, as they waited the year and a half for Shannon's body to develop to give the treatment a better chance, his son never expressed concern or fear. A school psychologist said he was more like a Navy SEAL than a grade school boy, focused on the mission.

      Finally, when the surgery arrived, the doctors drilled into two spots in his lower back to remove the marrow. He went home that night. Shannon was supposed to be in the hospital for 30 to 60 days; the procedure was such a success, she left in exactly 28 and hasn't looked back.

      She doesn't remember much about that time in her life. "I found a letter in my room that he wrote me when I was in the hospital a few years later," she said in a phone interview from her home. "How he was hoping that I'd get better. All of his classmates wrote cards, too."

      She is not cured. She still needs regular checkups because people with Fanconi Anemia have a higher rate of cancer occurrences at a young age, especially in the head and neck. Still, given where this started, her mother Jeanne Simonson said, "I have to pinch myself all the time and say, 'How the heck did we get here?'"

      She laughed over the phone and credits "Scotty's superstar NFL bone marrow" for saving her daughter. But even that part, the NFL journey, was a ride that few saw coming.  

      Division 2 to the Super Bowl 

      Scott Simonson Jr. played at Middletown South, a powerhouse that has produced several NFL players, including one, Knowshon Moreno, who played in a Super Bowl. But the lifelong Giants fan, who attended dozens of games with his father growing up, was hardly a star. 

      After battling injuries and illness, he didn't get much playing time as a receiver until his senior year, and by then, the Division 1 college scouts had looked elsewhere. So Simonson took his best offer: at tiny Assumption College, a liberal arts school in Worcester, Mass., that only produced one NFL player in its history (and that was a place-kicker for a single game). 

      It didn't take long for the coaches at the Division 2 school to know that they had struck recruiting gold. Simonson had grown into a 6-foot-5, 250-pound tight end with great hands, a talent that was virtually unheard of at that level. 

      "I didn't really know (the NFL was possible) until junior year of college when I started having scouts from teams showing up," he said. "I kind of thought, 'Maybe I could do something with this.'"

      MORE: How Peyton Manning plans to share Super Bowl 50 with Eli

      A big break came when, with the help of Rutgers legend Marco Battaglia, Simonson earned an invitation to Rutgers pro day. The Broncos had interest. So did the Chargers and Raiders. He signed with the Raiders as an undrafted free agent, and while he became a valuable special teams player and saw some action at tight end, he was cut in June. 

      That's when he received an unexpected call: The Panthers wanted him to come to Charlotte, N.C., for a workout. He impressed them enough to earn a spot at training camp, then on the practice squad, and then on the active roster midway through the season.

      He had no idea he was boarding a high-speed train to a shot at NFL history. That's just how it works sometimes. A player might spend 15 years as a pro without a shot at a championship, but Simonson gets one in his second year. 

      "Wow," he said during the mayhem that is Super Bowl Opening Night. "I'm just very fortunate, very happy with the opportunity and where my football career has gone. There have be some ups and downs, but this really would be something." 

      Is it karma for what he did as a boy 15 years ago? His father has no idea. He just knows that no matter what happens in Super Bowl 50 or beyond, his son is already the MVP of his family.
