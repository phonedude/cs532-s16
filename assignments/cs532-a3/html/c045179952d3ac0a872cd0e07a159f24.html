What Teach for America Has Learned

In its 25 years of existence, Teach for America has faced various challenges and become a better organization as a result.

A milestone for Teach for America.

By Sara Mead Feb. 6, 2016, at 10:56 a.m. + More

This weekend, thousands of Teach For America corps members and alumni gather to celebrate the organization's 25th anniversary. This milestone comes at a crucial turning point for Teach For America. After 15 years of rapid growth, the number of Teach For America corps members shrank in each of the last two years. The number of college students applying to Teach For America has fallen, and the organization faces intensified opposition on college campuses and in some communities. Yet while Teach For America's detractors may see its recent difficulties as validating their critiques, this is hardly the first time Teach For America has faced challenges.

In its first decade, Teach For America struggled to secure funds and credibility, often teetering on the verge of insolvency. Just as the organization gained financial stability in the early 2000s – and an independent evaluation demonstrated its corps members' impacts – the No Child Left Behind Act's "highly qualified teacher" provisions posed a crucial threat to its ability to place teachers. Later in the decade, Teach For America grew rapidly, but the pace of growth took a toll on its internal culture and staff morale. At each of these junctures, however, Teach For America leaders responded to adversity by taking stock and changing course where necessary. As a result, they emerged from these challenges with a stronger organization and better programs.

Last year, my colleagues Carolyn Chuong and Caroline Goodson and I worked with Teach For America to document its growth over the past 15 years – what it's done well, what it hasn't, and what others, as well as Teach For America itself can learn from these experiences. While the resulting report represents our completely independent analysis, we also recognize that we're not entirely unbiased observers: Many of our Bellwether colleagues and clients – not to mention several personal friends – are Teach For America alumni, with a wide variety of experiences with and perspectives on the organization.

Yet whatever your perspective on Teach For America, there's much worth learning from its experience over the past 25 years – both for other organizations and the education field as a whole.

If you pursue them, they will come. 

Teach For America's history demonstrates the importance of actively and intentionally recruiting talented young people into education. For most of its history, American public education relied on captive audiences of women and minority teachers who had few other professional career options. Yet as other options have opened up for talented women and minorities, the education field has continued to rely on teachers to come to it, rather than actively recruiting people to become teachers. Wendy Kopp's original vision for Teach For America grew out the recognition that no one was recruiting top college students to become teachers the way that investment banks and consulting firms pursued them. Teach For America's recruiting efforts made education a viable and exciting career option for thousands of young people who had never before thought of teaching. Over the past 25 years, Teach For America has invested incredible resources in improving and refining the way it identifies, recruits and selects prospective teachers. States, school districts and teacher preparation programs could learn from these approaches and from Teach For America's research on the traits and skills that predict corps member success. Yet even amid growing teacher shortages, relatively few states and districts recruit potential teachers as actively or intentionally as Teach For America does.

[READ: Teachers Need a Raise]

Organizations matter. 

Teach For America learned early on that even the best ideas are only as good as the organizations that implement them. The nascent organization's early struggles led founder Wendy Kopp and subsequent leaders to prioritize management and building a strong organization. In its second decade, this emphasis on management and strong internal systems enabled Teach For America to maintain and improve quality while growing rapidly. Yet education policies often ignore organizational capacity, and philanthropic funders are reluctant to invest in it. Advancing educational progress over the next 25 years will require much greater focus on organizational health and capacity. Growing organizations can also learn from the pros and cons of the various organizational structures that Teach For America has adopted as it had evolved – from lean and mean early on, to offering highly structured central supports in the mid-2000s, to a more flexible structure today.

Self-reflection is essential. 

Teach For America has been able to learn from its mistakes because it has developed a highly self-reflective – at times even self-critical – culture. Teach For America staff are constantly collecting information and data on how they're doing, analyzing performance and looking for opportunities to improve. That willingness to admit mistakes or weaknesses is an essential pre-condition for improving. But in our polarized educational debate, it's too often lacking. To provide our children the education they deserve, everyone involved in education practice and policy – both those who identify with the "education reform" agenda and their critics – needs to be much more self-reflective, open to criticism and willing to question deeply held beliefs and assumptions in response to new information. Teach For America's evolution over the past 25 years offers numerous illustrations of what this looks like in practice.

Past is not always prologue, of course. The fact that Teach For America has emerged stronger from past challenges is no guarantee it will do so again. But as the organization's current leaders set a course for the next 25 years, learning from the experience of the past 25 – both the specific actions Teach For America's leaders took in response to challenges and the process of self-reflection through which they arrived at those actions – is essential.
