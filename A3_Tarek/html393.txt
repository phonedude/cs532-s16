			<h1 class="entry-title">
					Business Sales and Capex Weaknesses Are Bad News for U.S. Jobs			</h1>

		<h2 class="entry-meta">
		by <a href="http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/author/constantin-gurdgiev/" title="Posts by Constantin Gurdgiev" rel="author">Constantin Gurdgiev</a>&nbsp;&bull;&nbsp;<time class="published updated" datetime="2016-02-10">February 10, 2016</time>	</h2>

	    <div class="entry-content">
		    <!-- Simple Share Buttons Adder (6.1.0) simplesharebuttons.com --><div class="ssba ssba-wrap"><div style="text-align:left"><a data-site="" class="ssba_twitter_share" href="http://twitter.com/share?url=http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/business-sales-and-capex-weaknesses-are-bad-news-for-u-s-jobs/&amp;text=Business+Sales+and+Capex+Weaknesses+Are+Bad+News+for+U.S.+Jobs+"  target="_blank" ><img src="http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/wp-content/plugins/simple-share-buttons-adder/buttons/simple/twitter.png" title="Twitter" class="ssba ssba-img" alt="Tweet about this on Twitter" /></a><a data-site="" class="ssba_facebook_share" href="http://www.facebook.com/sharer.php?u=http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/business-sales-and-capex-weaknesses-are-bad-news-for-u-s-jobs/"  target="_blank" ><img src="http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/wp-content/plugins/simple-share-buttons-adder/buttons/simple/facebook.png" title="Facebook" class="ssba ssba-img" alt="Share on Facebook" /></a><a data-site="linkedin" class="ssba_linkedin_share ssba_share_link" href="http://www.linkedin.com/shareArticle?mini=true&amp;url=http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/business-sales-and-capex-weaknesses-are-bad-news-for-u-s-jobs/"  target="_blank" ><img src="http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/wp-content/plugins/simple-share-buttons-adder/buttons/simple/linkedin.png" title="LinkedIn" class="ssba ssba-img" alt="Share on LinkedIn" /></a><a data-site="print" class="ssba_print ssba_share_link" href="#" onclick="window.print()"><img src="http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/wp-content/plugins/simple-share-buttons-adder/buttons/simple/print.png" title="Print" class="ssba ssba-img" alt="Print this page" /></a><a data-site="email" class="ssba_email_share" href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection#f8c78b8d9a929d9b8cc5ba8d8b91969d8b8bd8ab99949d8bd899969cd8bb99889d80d8af9d9993969d8b8b9d8bd8b98a9dd8ba999cd8b69d8f8bd89e978ad8add6abX. Jobs&amp;body=%20http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/business-sales-and-capex-weaknesses-are-bad-news-for-u-s-jobs/"><img src="http://davidstockmanscontracorner.com/wp-content/plugins/simple-share-buttons-adder/buttons/simple/email.png" title="Email" class="ssba ssba-img" alt="Email this to someone" /></a></div></div><div class="pf-content"><div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;">
<p>In a note from February 4, Moody Analytics have this two key messages about the U.S. economy, none pleasant:</p>
<ul style="text-align: left;">
<li>Business sales are ‘mediocre’ outside energy sector, so that jobs growth singled by business sales outside energy sector should be slowing; and</li>
<li>Capex slowdown is about to smack jobs growth even further to the downside.</li>
</ul>
<p>Take their numbers with a gulp of some oxygen.</p>
<p><b>Point 1: Business sales</b></p>
<p>The old-fashioned statistics don’t quite fudge stuff as well as the more modern hoopla about users, unique visits and signups deployed in the ICT sector. So here we go:</p>
<p>“Don’t fall into the trap of believing all is well outside of oil &amp; gas. According to Bloomberg News, the 52% of the S&amp;P 500 that has reported for 2015’s final quarter incurred over-year setbacks of -4.9% for sales and -5.7% for operating income. To a considerable degree, the declines were skewed lower by annual plunges of -34.2% for the sales and -64.2% for the operating profits of the latest sample’s 18 energy companies. For the 53% of the S&amp;P 500’s non-energy companies that have reported for Q4-2015, sales barely rose by 0.6% annually, while the 2.6% increase by operating income fell considerably short of long-term profits growth of 6.5%.”</p>
<p>You’ve heard it right: <b>in a recovery the U.S. is having, sales are up 0.6% y/y.</b> Know of any real business that lives off something other than sales? I don’t.</p>
<p>Based on the Commerce Department broad estimate of business sales “that sums the sales of manufacturers, retailers and wholesalers. …even after excluding sales of identifiable energy products, what I refer to as core business sales posted annual increases of merely +2.1% for 2015 and +1.0% for Q4-2015”.</p>
<p>“…payrolls have been surprisingly resilient to the slowest growth by business sales excluding energy products since Q4-2009.” But, based on 3-mo average payrolls correlation with 12-mo average business sales data (estimated by Moody’s at 0.87), 2015 figures for sales suggest “…<b>the average increase of private sector payrolls may descend from 2015’s 213,000 new jobs per month to 42,000 new jobs per month.</b> Unless core business sales accelerate, 2016’s macro risks are most definitely to the downside.”</p>
<p>A handy chart:</p>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;" href="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-_FJBCORmaYM/Vrp5lcKkQSI/AAAAAAAAbCU/rKdyCmIXr5g/s1600/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-09%2Bat%2B15.21.18.png"><img src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-_FJBCORmaYM/Vrp5lcKkQSI/AAAAAAAAbCU/rKdyCmIXr5g/s400/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-09%2Bat%2B15.21.18.png" alt="" width="400" height="245" border="0" /></a></div>
<p><b>Point 2: Capex headwind for jobs growth</b></p>
<p>“Business outlays on staff and capital spending are highly correlated. Over the past 33 years, the yearly percent change of payrolls revealed a strong correlation of 0.84 with the yearly percent change of real business investment spending.”</p>
<p>So, <b>based on 2015 yearly increase in capital spending private sector payrolls “should have approximated 0.8% instead of the actual 1.9%</b>. In other words, Q4-2015’s 1.6% yearly increase by real business investment spending favored a 91,000 average monthly increase by 2015’s payrolls, which was considerably less than the actual average monthly increase of 221,000 jobs.”</p>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;" href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-M_f5K-lxNeA/Vrp5lZrjwkI/AAAAAAAAbCY/2WEnToLyidM/s1600/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-09%2Bat%2B15.22.07.png"><img src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-M_f5K-lxNeA/Vrp5lZrjwkI/AAAAAAAAbCY/2WEnToLyidM/s400/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-09%2Bat%2B15.22.07.png" alt="" width="400" height="277" border="0" /></a></div>
<p>All of which puts into perspective what I wrote recently about the U.S. non farm payroll numbers here: <a href="http://trueeconomics.blogspot.com/2016/02/5216-three-facts-from-us-labor-markets.html">http://trueeconomics.blogspot.com/2016/02/5216-three-facts-from-us-labor-markets.html</a></p>
<p>You really have to wonder, just how long can the U.S. economy continue raising the bar on additional bar staff hiring before choking on shortages of sales and capital investment?</p>
