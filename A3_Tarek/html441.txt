	<header>
	<h1 class="title entry-title">Emancipation Proclamation What Is It?</h1>	</header>
<div class="post-meta"><span class="small">By</span> <span class="author vcard"><span class="fn"><a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/author/admin/" title="Posts by &lt;a href=&quot;http://www.michaeloart.com/byline/michael-owens/&quot; rel=&quot;tag&quot;&gt;Michael Owens&lt;/a&gt;" rel="author"><a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/byline/michael-owens/" rel="tag">Michael Owens</a></a></span></span> <span class="small">on</span> <abbr class="date time published updated" title="2015-02-24T09:00:24-0500">February 24, 2015</abbr>  <span class="small">in</span> <span class="categories"><a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/category/inspiration/" title="View all items in Inspiration">Inspiration</a>, <a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/category/politics/" title="View all items in Politics">Politics</a></span>  </div>
	<section class="entry">
<p><a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/emancipation-proclamation/eman-proc-doc/" rel="attachment wp-att-6371"><img class=" alignleft wp-image-6371 size-full" title="The Emancipation Proclamation" src="http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/eman-proc-doc.jpg" alt="The Emancipation Proclamation" width="300" height="383" srcset="http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/eman-proc-doc-235x300.jpg 235w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/eman-proc-doc-250x319.jpg 250w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/eman-proc-doc.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>The Emancipation Proclamation was<strong> an executive order issued by President Abraham Lincoln.</strong> As of January 1, 1863 <strong>all slaves held within the 10 states not under Union control were declared to be free.</strong></p>
<p>The order did not free all of the slaves. It <strong>had no effect on the status of slaves held in states loyal to the union.</strong> But it did apply to 3 million enslaved Africans. There were 4 million slaves total at the time.</p>
<h3></h3>
<h3><strong>Specifics</strong></h3>
<p><strong>The proclamation was not a law passed by congress.</strong> It was limited in scope. But it did apply to all segments of the executive branch. This included the U.S. Army and Navy.</p>
<p>On September 22, 1862 Lincoln issued a preliminary executive order. This was a warning to all rebellious southern states. They had until new year&#8217;s day 1863 to return to the union. If not their slaves would be declared free. None of them returned so the proclamation took effect.</p>
<p>The proclamation paved the way for ex-slaves to join the U.S. military.  It ordered the military to recognize the freedom of ex-slaves and not return them to their &#8220;masters.&#8221;</p>
<p>It did not outlaw slavery. It did not reimburse their former masters.  <strong>But it did acknowledge ending slavery as one of the goals of the war. </strong>That is a very important point to remember.</p>
<p><strong>Before the proclamation escaped slaves were returned to their &#8220;masters&#8221;.</strong> If that wasn&#8217;t possible they were held in camps as &#8220;contraband&#8221; to be returned later. This was in keeping with the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850.</p>
<h3></h3>
<h3></h3>
<h3><strong>Immediate Fallout</strong></h3>
<p><strong>The Emancipation Proclamation had several effects.</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>It enraged white Southerners who feared a race war.</li>
<li>It angered some Northern Democrats.</li>
<li>It boosted anti-slavery forces.</li>
<li>European forces that wanted to intervene on the Southern side were held in place. (Much of Europe had outlawed slavery).</li>
<li>It buoyed the spirits of blacks both slave and free.</li>
<li>It led many slaves to escape. (They hoped to reach Union lines and freedom).</li>
<li>The proclamation widened the goals of the Civil War.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Lincoln&#8217;s main concern at the beginning was to preserve the Union. </strong>Slavery was a major issue. But <strong>freeing the slaves was not one of his goals.</strong></p>
<p><strong>The Proclamation changed that</strong>. It made freeing the slaves an explicit goal of the Union war effort. This was<strong> a step toward outlawing slavery and granting full citizenship to ex-slaves.</strong></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Legal Basis For The Proclamation</h3>
<p>The 1787 U.S. Constitution never mentioned the word &#8220;slave.&#8221; It referred to slaves as &#8220;unfree persons.&#8221; But slavery was still dealt with in the document.</p>
<p><strong>The Three-Fifths Compromise.</strong> This rule allowed slaves to be counted in the U.S. Census as 3/5 of a person. Whites of course, were counted as a whole person.</p>
<p>In this way Southern states were given more representation is Congress than their citizen population warranted. Great Southern influence over the nation&#8217;s affairs was guaranteed.</p>
<p><strong>The Fugitive Slave Clause</strong>. This clause meant anyone held &#8220;in service&#8221; in one state could not  be freed simply by escaping to another state.</p>
<p><strong>Article 1, section 9</strong> allowed states to ban the &#8220;importation of persons&#8221;after the year 1808. In other words states could ban importing slaves from Africa.</p>
<p><strong>The Fifth Amendment</strong> was interpreted as a defense of slavery. This amendment bans the government from taking anyone&#8217;s property without due process. Slaves were understood to be &#8220;property&#8221;.</p>
<p>This provided the legal basis for the infamous Dred Scott decision. Mr. Scott was returned to slavery because he was considered to be &#8220;property&#8221;. Therefore he had no rights.</p>
<p>President Lincoln knew <strong>slavery could be outlawed only through the U.S. Constitution</strong>. And then only by individual states.</p>
<p>But against the backdrop of the civil war <strong>Lincoln issued the proclamation under his authority as Commander in Chief.</strong> He justified his act as a war tactic. He assumed martial powers over the states which had tried to leave the Union.</p>
<p>He didn&#8217;t claim martial power over the slave states still loyal to the Union. Missouri. Kentucky. Maryland and Delaware. West Virginia was still in the process of banning slavery.</p>
<p><strong>The Emancipation Proclamation was never legally challenged.</strong>  To put an end to slavery Lincoln pushed for passage of the 13th Amendment. The amendment passed on January 31, 1865. It was ratified December 6, 1865.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3><strong>Who The Proclamation Covered</strong></h3>
<p><strong>The Proclamation applied only to the 10 states that were still in rebellion in 1863.</strong> It didn&#8217;t cover the nearly half a million slaves in other states.<a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/emancipation-proclamation/attachment/00035671/" rel="attachment wp-att-6375"><img class=" alignright wp-image-6375 size-full" title="Areas of the South affected by the Emancipation Proclamation" src="http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/00035671.jpg" alt="Areas of the South affected by the Emancipation Proclamation" width="380" height="234" srcset="http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/00035671-300x185.jpg 300w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/00035671-250x154.jpg 250w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/00035671.jpg 380w" sizes="(max-width: 380px) 100vw, 380px" /></a></p>
<p><strong>Slaves in Missouri, Kentucky, Maryland or Delaware were still not free</strong>. This was done later. It took separate state and federal actions to accomplish this.</p>
<p>This is were things get a little confusing. By 1863 most of Tennessee had returned to the Union. So it wasn&#8217;t named in the proclamation.</p>
<p>Virginia was named. But there were 48 counties in the process of becoming the new state of West Virginia. They were left out. Seven other counties were also exempt.</p>
<p>Thirteen Louisiana parishes plus New Orleans were also exempted. Most of this area was under Union control. These exemptions left an additional 300,000 people still in bondage.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s easy to see why <strong>the Proclamation was ridiculed for only freeing slaves over which the Union had no control.</strong> Still the proclamation cleared up the status of fugitive slaves. <strong>Nearly 50,000 blacks were freed the day it went into effect.</strong> The South had to take it seriously.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3><strong>The Impact of Freedom</strong></h3>
<p><a href="http://www.michaeloart.com/emancipation-proclamation/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times-jpg-crop-rtstory-large/" rel="attachment wp-att-6381"><img class=" alignleft wp-image-6381 size-medium" title="Harriet Tubman with slaves rescued before the Emancipation Proclamation" src="http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times.jpg.CROP_.rtstory-large-590x332.jpg" alt="Harriet Tubman with slaves rescued before the Emancipation Proclamation" width="590" height="332" srcset="http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times.jpg.CROP_.rtstory-large-300x169.jpg 300w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times.jpg.CROP_.rtstory-large-590x332.jpg 590w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times.jpg.CROP_.rtstory-large-250x141.jpg 250w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times.jpg.CROP_.rtstory-large-500x281.jpg 500w, http://www.michaeloart.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/640pxharriet_tubman_with_rescued_slaves_new_york_times.jpg.CROP_.rtstory-large.jpg 670w" sizes="(max-width: 590px) 100vw, 590px" /></a>It&#8217;s a very common belief that the Proclamation did not free a single slave right away. That&#8217;s a false impression. There was great joy among blacks the day it took effect.</p>
<p>According to eyewitnesses there were celebrations in various places. Hilton Head, South Carolina. Port Royal, South Carolina. <strong>Ex-slaves celebrated as soon as they learned of their new legal status.</strong></p>
<p>No one knows the exact number of slaves freed right away.  But the most common estimate is 20,000.</p>
<p><strong>There was a Union occupied zone where freedom began at once on January 1, 1863.</strong></p>
<p>This included parts of eastern North Carolina. The Mississippi Valley. Northern Alabama. Virginia&#8217;s Shenandoah Valley. A large part of Arkansas. The Sea Islands of Georgia and South Carolina.</p>
<p><strong>The Proclamation was enforced along the way as Union soldiers advanced into the South.</strong> Slaves often left their their masters with help from Union soldiers.</p>
<p><strong>Booker T. Washington, as a boy of 9 in Virginia, remembered the day in early 1865:</strong></p>
<p><em>As the great day drew nearer, there was more singing in the slave quarters than usual. It was bolder, had more ring, and lasted later into the night. Most of the verses of the plantation songs had some reference to freedom&#8230;. Some man who seemed to be a stranger (a United States officer, I presume) made a little speech and then read a rather long paper—the Emancipation Proclamation, I think. After the reading we were told that we were all free, and could go when and where we pleased. My mother, who was standing by my side, leaned over and kissed her children, while tears of joy ran down her cheeks. She explained to us what it all meant, that this was the day for which she had been so long praying, but fearing that she would never live to see.</em></p>
<p><strong>The Proclamation allowed freedom to happen without violence between masters and slaves.</strong> It meant a shift in the war objectives of the North. Reuniting the nation was no longer the only goal.</p>
<p><strong>It was a major step toward the ultimate removal of slavery in the United States.</strong> A &#8220;new birth of freedom&#8221;.</p>
<p><strong>Slaves were a huge part of the southern war effort.</strong> They produced and prepared food. Sewed uniforms. Repaired railroads. Worked on farms and in factories. Shipping yards and mines. Built fortifications. Served as hospital workers and common laborers.</p>
<p><strong>News of the Proclamation spread rapidly by word of mouth</strong>, giving hope of freedom. Although there was some confusion, the news encouraged thousands to escape to Union lines.</p>
<p>George Washington Albright, a teenage slave in Mississippi, recalled that like many of his fellow slaves, his father escaped to join Union forces. According to Albright, <strong>plantation owners tried to keep the Proclamation from slaves but news of it came through the &#8220;grapevine&#8221;.</strong> The news was brought to secret slave meetings at plantations throughout the region.</p>
<h3></h3>
<h3><strong>Legacy</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Martin Luther King spoke of the Proclamation many times.</strong> He made a speech on its 100th anniversary. He said it was one of the primary documents of American civilization. The other being the Declaration of Independence.</p>
<p>King spoke of America&#8217;s history.<strong> How we never lived up to the principles found in these documents.</strong></p>
<p>He concluded there was only one way to honor the Proclamation. That is to <strong>&#8220;make its declarations of freedom real.&#8221;</strong></p>
<p>King mentioned the Proclamation in his most famous speech. His &#8220;I have a dream&#8221; speech. From the steps of the Lincoln Memorial at the 1963 March on Washington. He said these words.</p>
<p><em>&#8220;Five score years ago, a great American, in whose symbolic shadow we stand, signed the Emancipation Proclamation. This momentous decree came as a great beacon light of hope to millions of Negro slaves who had been seared in the flames of withering injustice.</em></p>
<p><em>It came as a joyous daybreak to end the long night of captivity. But one hundred years later, we must face the tragic fact that the Negro is still not free. One hundred years later, the life of the Negro is still sadly crippled by the manacles of segregation and the chains of discrimination.&#8221;</em></p>
<p><em> </em></p>
