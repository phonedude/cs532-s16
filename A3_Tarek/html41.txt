				<p>Sens. Ted Cruz (TX) and Marco Rubio (FL) left the South Carolina campaign trail in their pursuit of the Republican nomination to get back to Washington, D.C. on Wednesday.</p>
<p>While both candidates held morning events, other stops in The First in The South primary states were <a rel="nofollow" href="http://floridapolitics.com/archives/201211-marco-rubio-to-miss-full-day-of-campaigning-in-south-carolina-to-return-to-the-senate-for-a-day" target="_blank">canceled or cut</a> short in the afternoon. Rubio shortened a <a rel="nofollow" href="http://www.thestate.com/news/politics-government/politics-columns-blogs/the-buzz/article59678426.html" target="_blank">rally in Columbia</a> from its planned hour and half to 30 minutes (he only spoke 17 minutes) so he and <a href="http://www.ijreview.com/2016/02/527846-tim-scott-endorsing-marco-rubio/" target="_blank">campaign partner</a> Sen. Tim Scott (R-SC) could head back to the Senate.</p>
<p>Sen. Bernie Sanders (D-VT), also campaigning in South Carolina, did not drop his GOTV efforts to vote in D.C.</p>
<p>In a Republican presidential primary heavily influenced by foreign policy, a vote on <a rel="nofollow" href="http://www.reuters.com/article/us-northkorea-usa-sanctions-idUSKCN0VJ2OS?feedType=RSS&amp;feedName=topNews" target="_blank">curbing North Korea</a> is what pulled two candidates off the campaign trail.</p>
<div id="attachment_535097" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="http://static.ijreview.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/rubio-senate.jpg"><img class="size-large wp-image-535097" src="http://static.ijreview.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/rubio-senate-1024x683.jpg" alt="WASHINGTON, DC - FEBRUARY 10: Sen. Marco Rubio (R-FL), a Republican presidential hopeful, makes a quick exit after voting on the Senate floor at the Capitol on February 10, 2016 in Washington, D.C. The Senate voted Wednesday night on a North Korean sanctions bill. (Photo by Gabriella Demczuk/Getty Images)" width="1024" height="683" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Sen. Marco Rubio (R-FL), a Republican presidential hopeful, makes a quick exit after voting on the Senate floor at the Capitol on February 10, 2016 in Washington, D.C.; Image Credit: Gabriella Demczuk/Getty Images</p></div>
<p>The bill (<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.congress.gov/bill/114th-congress/senate-bill/2144" target="_blank">S-2144</a>), meant to toughen policies towards the Democratic People&#8217;s Republic of Korea, ended up with unanimous support in the Senate (96-0). It focuses on sanctioning anyone participating in or aiding North Korea&#8217;s development of weapons of mass destruction and human rights violations.</p>
<p>But the bill also serves as a statement to the international community, particularly the UN. Sen. Bob Corker (R-TN) chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee explained:</p>
<blockquote><p>&#8220;China, the very entity that could do something about this, is blocking the U.N. Security Council&#8217;s action toward this being done on a multilateral basis.&#8221;</p></blockquote>
<p>The &#8220;North Korea Sanctions and Policy Enhancement Act&#8221; follows a recent missile test by the errant nation and a continuous ramping up of rhetoric.</p>
<div id="attachment_535103" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="http://static.ijreview.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/la-north-korea-submarine-missile-test-20150509.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-535103" src="http://static.ijreview.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/la-north-korea-submarine-missile-test-20150509.jpg" alt="Footage released by North Korea of attempted missile launch in 2015; Image Credit: YouTube" width="693" height="390" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Footage released by North Korea of attempted missile launch in 2015; Image Credit: YouTube</p></div>
<p>The latest test actually took place during <a href="http://www.ijreview.com/2016/02/531608-right-as-republicans-hit-the-debate-stage-north-korea-test-nuclear-rocket-over-the-pacific/" target="_blank">a Republican debate</a>, which might have added additional urgency to the decision by the two GOP candidates to attend the vote. Both junior senators could also be using the vote to <a rel="nofollow" href="http://www.politifact.com/florida/statements/2015/oct/28/marco-rubio/when-attacked-missed-votes-marco-rubio-calls-out-b/" target="_blank">quell attacks</a> against their <a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.ijreview.com/2016/01/513935-rand-pauls-audit-the-fed-bill-was-defeated-hes-blaming-one-2016-candidate-in-particular/" target="_blank">attendance record</a> ahead of the next debate <a rel="nofollow" href="http://www.cnn.com/2016/02/10/politics/new-hampshire-primary-results/" target="_blank">on Saturday</a>.</p>
<p>Sanders probably isn&#8217;t feeling the same heat about his voting record. The Vermont senator has a long history on Capitol Hill and has not made <a href="http://www.ijreview.com/2016/02/530447-bernie-sanders-foreign-policy-position-paper-new-hampshire/" target="_blank">foreign policy</a> central to his campaign, despite his <a href="http://www.ijreview.com/2016/02/527645-hillary-clinton-wins-iowa/" target="_blank">repeated elbowing</a> of Democratic opponent Hillary Clinton over her vote on the Iraq War.</p>
<p>The White House has not commented on the Senate bill, but a similar bill (<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.congress.gov/bill/114th-congress/house-bill/757" target="_blank">HR-757</a>) passed the House last month. Reuters notes that the two bill are expected to be aligned quickly and then signed by President Obama into law.</p>
